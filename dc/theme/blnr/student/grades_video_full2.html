Modes of nutrition

Nutrition is an important life process by which all living organisms take food and utilise it to get energy.

There are two types of nutrition modes: Autotrophic nutrition and Heterotrophic nutrition.

Autotrophic nutrition is the mode of nutrition, in which organisms make organic food themselves from simple inorganic substances.

These organisms are hence called autotrophs.

Green plants are the examples of autotrophs.

Heterotrophic nutrition is the mode of nutrition in which organisms depend upon other organisms to survive.

Accordingly, these organisms are called heterotrophs.

Human beings and tigers are the examples of heterotrophs.

Photosynthesis

Photosynthesis is a process by which green plants synthesise their own food from the carbon dioxide and water, in the presence of chlorophyll and sunlight.

Raw materials for photosynthesis

In plants, water, minerals, and carbon dioxide are raw materials for the synthesis of food.

How do plants obtain these raw materials from their surroundings?

How do they transport them to the food factories of the plant?

Water and dissolved minerals are absorbed directly from the soil through roots, by the means of many small vessels.

These vessels run throughout the root, the stem, the branches, and the leaves.

They form a continuous path or passage for water and minerals to reach the leaf.

Likewise, carbon dioxide from the air is taken in through the tiny pores, present on both surfaces of the leaves.

These pores are called stomata.

They are surrounded by ‘guard cells’.

Products of photosynthesis

After reaching the leaves, carbon dioxide and water react in the presence of sunlight to form carbohydrates.

The carbohydrates are ultimately converted into starch which gets stored in the different parts of the plant.

Therefore, the process of photosynthesis can be represented as:

Carbon dioxide plus water, in the presence of chlorophyll and sunlight gives carbohydrate plus oxygen.

The Significance of photosynthesis

Photosynthesis is an important and vital process for life on this planet.

This process helps in the conversion of solar energy into organic matter.

Hence, photosynthesis links the physical and biological world.

It is the primary source of organic food for all living forms, either directly or indirectly.

It also helps in purifying air and maintaining the balance of oxygen and carbon dioxide in the ecosystem.

Heterotrophic mode of nutrition in plants

Heterotrophic mode of nutrition is the mode of nutrition in which plants which do not have chlorophyll and can not synthesise food on their own, derive food from other plants and animals.

Plants which use this mode of nutrition are known as heterotrophs.

There are four types of heterotrophic plants:

Parasitic plants

Saprophytic plants

Symbiotic plants

Insectivorous plants

Parasitic plants

Parasitic plants are plants which obtain their food directly from other plants.

The plants from which they obtain food are called hosts.

Cuscuta is an example of a parasitic plant.

It coils around the stem and branches as a yellow, tubular structure and takes food from the host plant.

Saprophytic plants

You may have seen fluffy umbrella-like patches growing on rotting wood during rainy season.

These organisms are commonly called fungi.

Fungi get their nutrition from dead, decaying matter.

To get nutrients, they secrete a digestive juice and converts it into a solution.

This mode of nutrition is called saprotrophic nutrition and organisms using this mode of nutrition are called saprotrophs or saprophytes.

Fungi is an example of saprotrophs.

Symbiotic plants

Symbiotic relationship is the relationship between two dissimilar organisms, in which both are living together and sharing shelter and nutrients with each other.

The organisms involved in this kind of relationship are called symbionts.

Lichen is a good example of a symbiotic relationship.

It is the association between algae and fungi.

In this kind of relationship, algae provide food to fungi and fungi provide protection, absorbed water and minerals to algae.

Insectivorous plants

There are a few plants which can trap insects and digest them to get nutrition.

For example, pitcher plants.

These plants can synthesise their own food by photosynthesis, but they grow in nitrogen deficient soils.

In order to fulfil their nitrogen requirements, they eat small insects.

These are called insectivorous plants.

Rhizobium- A symbiont

Plants require a lot of nitrogen to make proteins.

Our atmosphere contains 78% nitrogen but plants cannot use it directly.

In the root nodules of gram, peas, and other legumes, a bacterium called Rhizobium lives that can take atmospheric nitrogen and convert it into a soluble form.

In return, the plant provides food and shelter to the bacteria.

Thus we can say that, Rhizobium shows a symbiotic relationship.

With this, we come to the end of this module. You are now aware of:
  • The process by which all living organisms ingest food and utilise it to get energy is called nutrition
  • Plants obtain their food through two modes: Autotrophic and Heterotrophic nutrition
  • In autotrophic mode of nutrition, plants synthesise their own food through the process of photosynthesis and are called autotrophs
  • Photosynthesis is a process by which green plants synthesise their own food using carbon dioxide and water, in the presence of chlorophyll and sunlight
  • In heterotrophic mode of nutrition, plants do not synthesise their own food but derive food from other plants and animals and are called heterotrophs
  • Parasitic plants obtain their food directly from other plants, which are known as hosts
  • Saprophytic plants get their nutrients from dead and decaying matter, by secreting a digestive juice which converts this matter into a solution
  • Symbiotic relationship is the relationship between two dissimilar organisms, in which both are living together and sharing shelter and nutrients with each other
  • Insectivorous plants can synthesise their own food but they fulfil their nitrogen requirement by trapping or eating small insects
